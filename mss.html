The Möbius Strip in Science

In manufacturing:


There have been several technical applications for the Möbius strip. Giant Möbius strips have been used as conveyor belts that last longer because the entire surface area of the belt gets the same amount of wear, and as continuous-loop recording tapes (to double the playing time). Möbius strips are common in the manufacture of fabric computer printer and typewriter ribbons, as they allow the ribbon to be twice as wide as the print head while using both half-edges evenly.

In electro-technology:

  • A device called a Möbius resistor is an electronic circuit element that has the property of cancelling its own inductive reactance. Nikola Tesla patented similar technology in the early 1900s: "Coil for Electro Magnets" was intended for use with his system of global transmission of electricity without wires.
  • Other applications include compact resonators with the resonance frequency which is half that of identically constructed linear coils, and usage in superconductors with a high transition temperature.

In chemistry/nanotechnology:

  • Charged particles, which were caught in the magnetic field of the earth, can move on a Möbius band: particles orbit in real space in the Earth’s geomagnetic tail, where the magnetic field differs by only 0.01 per cent. Just before becoming chaotic, the particles are found to move on a Möbius strip (a continuous band with a twist in it) in three-dimensional space. This then bifurcates (splits down the middle). Only by looking in true three-dimensional space in VR were we able to be sure that the orbits have the Möbius-strip topology. This then tells us what kind of mathematical description is needed to understand the problem.
  • Molecular knots with special characteristics (Knotane, Chirality)
  • Molecular engines
  • Graphene volume (nano-graphite) with new electronic characteristics, like helical magnetism
  • A special type of aromaticity, aptly named Möbius aromaticity
  • The cyclotide (cyclic protein) Kalata B1, active substance of the plant Oldenlandia affinis, contains Möbius topology for the peptide backbone.
